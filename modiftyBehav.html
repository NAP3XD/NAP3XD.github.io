Introduction

Introduce the problem I want to find out what modifiable health behaviors have the greatest outcome on heart disease. Cardiovascular disease (CVD) is the leading cause of mortality worldwide, but many of its risk factors are modifiable lifestyle choices. The purpose of this project is to explore which behaviors, such as alcohol consumption, smoking, and physical activity as these are most strongly associated with cardiovascular outcomes in a large patient dataset. Beyond simply building a classifier to predict CVD, the goal is to identify and compare lifestyle factors that individuals may be able to change in order to reduce their risk.

Introduce the data 7000 rows separated with ; age is in days (not years) Blood pressure columns are usually ap_hi (systolic) and ap_lo (diastolic) Categorical codes: cholesterol/gluc as 1 (normal), 2 (above), 3 (well above) Lifestyle flags: smoke, alco, active, and target cardio are binary Heights in cm, weights in kg

The dataset was obtained from Kaggle: Cardiovascular Disease Dataset . It contains 70,000 patient records with demographic, lifestyle, and clinical measures. Each row represents one patient, and the target variable cardio indicates whether the patient has cardiovascular disease (1 = present, 0 = not present).

Features include: Demographic: age (in days), gender (1 = female, 2 = male), height (cm), weight (kg) Clinical: ap_hi (systolic blood pressure), ap_lo (diastolic blood pressure), cholesterol (1 = normal, 2 = above normal, 3 = well above normal), gluc (same scale for glucose) Lifestyle: smoke (0/1), alco (0/1), active (0/1) Target: cardio (0 = no CVD, 1 = CVD present)

This combination of numerical, categorical, and binary variables makes the dataset well-suited for classification tasks and exploratory analysis of health risk factors.

Pre-processing the data Although the dataset had no missing values, several transformations and filters were applied to improve interpretability and ensure biological plausibility. Convert age from days to years: Dividing by 365.25 provides a clinically meaningful measure and makes comparisons easier.

Create BMI: Calculated as weight (kg) / heightÂ² (mÂ²). BMI is widely used to assess body composition and correlates with cardiovascular risk.

Create pulse pressure: Defined as systolic - diastolic blood pressure. Abnormally high or low values are linked to arterial stiffness and heart problems.

Filter implausible values: Removed rows with unrealistic measurements (e.g., height <130 cm, weight <35 kg, systolic <80 mmHg, diastolic <40 mmHg, negative pulse pressure, age <18 or >100). This ensures the analysis is based on physiologically credible data.

After preprocessing, the dataset retained the majority of rows, while eliminating records that could distort analysis or produce misleading insights.

Data Understanding/Visualization After preprocessing, the dataset contained over 65,000 patient records with a balanced mix of clinical, lifestyle, and demographic features. To better understand the data and connect it back to my main question, which modifiable lifestyle factors are most associated with cardiovascular disease. I began by examining the distributions of key variables and their relationships to the target. The derived variables behaved as expected. Age in years showed a wide distribution from young adults into the 70s and beyond, with most patients between 40 and 65 years old. BMI followed a bell-shaped curve centered around 25-30, indicating that overweight and obesity are common in this sample. Pulse pressure clustered around 40-50 mmHg, which is within the typical healthy range, though a noticeable tail of elevated values suggests a subset of patients with stiffer arteries or uncontrolled blood pressure. These distributions confirm that the dataset reflects realistic clinical patterns. Turning to lifestyle factors, I used bar charts to compare the prevalence of cardiovascular disease across alcohol use, smoking, and physical activity groups. Each bar was labeled with both the percentage of patients with CVD and the sample size, to avoid over-interpreting small categories. Interestingly, cardiovascular prevalence was only slightly higher among drinkers compared to non-drinkers, and the same pattern was seen for smokers versus non-smokers. By contrast, physical activity showed a clearer protective effect: inactive patients had a noticeably higher rate of CVD compared to those who reported being physically active. I also explored interactions between lifestyle factors. Two-way plots showed how combinations such as alcohol + smoking or alcohol + activity related to disease prevalence. For example, patients who both smoked and drank tended to have the highest CVD rates, while those who were active non-drinkers showed the lowest. However, sample size graphs revealed that some combinations (such as active drinkers who also smoke) were relatively rare in the dataset, making it important to interpret those results cautiously.

Finally, I checked the overall class balance of the target variable. Cardiovascular disease was present in roughly half the sample, which means the dataset is not severely imbalanced. This balance is useful because it reduces the need for aggressive resampling or weighting during modeling. Together, these visualizations provided an important first look at the data. They confirmed that the dataset is both plausible and informative, while also suggesting that physical activity may have a stronger relationship with cardiovascular outcomes than alcohol or smoking alone. At the same time, the inclusion of interaction effects and sample size checks highlights the complexity of lifestyle data and the need to approach conclusions with care. Storytelling When I first chose this dataset, I was drawn to its size and structure. With 70,000 records and clearly defined numeric and categorical features, it looked like it would be straightforward to work with. I hoped that by focusing on alcohol consumption, I could tell a simple and powerful story about how lifestyle choices influence cardiovascular disease. On paper, this seemed like the perfect dataset for a first data mining project. As I began exploring the data, however, I quickly realized that my initial assumptions didnâ€™t hold. The alcohol variable was binary, coded only as 0 or 1. This meant there was no way to distinguish between someone who has an occasional drink and someone who drinks heavily every day. On top of that, the vast majority of the sample reported not drinking at all, which left the drinking group small and statistically thin. Visualizations confirmed that any differences in cardiovascular disease prevalence between drinkers and non-drinkers were minimal and difficult to interpret. This was both frustrating and enlightening. On one hand, my original research question around alcohol could not be answered with the level of nuance I wanted. On the other hand, the process forced me to think critically about the limitations of real-world data. Instead of focusing solely on alcohol, I broadened my scope to other modifiable lifestyle factors such as smoking and physical activity. Interestingly, variables such as physical activity showed stronger and clearer associations with cardiovascular disease than alcohol did. In the end, my story is not just about the relationship between alcohol and health, but also about the process of discovery in data mining. Even when a dataset looks â€œcleanâ€ and well-structured, hidden limitations can make the work more challenging than expected. The lesson I take away is that good data science is not just about finding the answers we expect, but about adapting our questions to what the data can realistically tell us. Impact Section This project has both academic and public health value. From an academic perspective, the dataset provided a rich opportunity to practice the core steps of data mining: preprocessing, creating new features, exploring distributions, and evaluating patterns across subgroups. Even though the alcohol variable did not turn out to be as informative as I had hoped, the process taught me how to deal with limitations in real-world data and how to adjust my research questions when the original plan doesnâ€™t fit the evidence. From a public health standpoint, the analysis reinforced the importance of modifiable lifestyle factors. While alcohol and smoking did not show strong associations in this dataset, physical activity and weight-related measures such as BMI and obesity status were consistently linked to cardiovascular disease prevalence. Highlighting such factors matters because they represent choices that people can change, unlike fixed risk factors such as age or genetics. Even with its limitations, the dataset shows that promoting physical activity and healthy weight management may help reduce cardiovascular disease risk. At the same time, the project carries potential harms if the findings are taken out of context. Because alcohol intake is coded only as a binary variable, it treats all drinking behavior the same, from occasional social use to heavy daily consumption. This oversimplification could mislead readers into underestimating or misunderstanding alcoholâ€™s role in heart health. Similarly, focusing only on lifestyle could inadvertently stigmatize individuals, implying that cardiovascular disease is entirely their fault while ignoring the strong influence of genetics, stress, environment, and access to healthcare. Finally, because the data is observational and cross-sectional, it can only show correlations, not causation. That is to say, anyone using these results as proof of cause-and-effect would be overreaching. There are also perspectives missing from the dataset itself. Important health factors such as diet, socioeconomic status, medication use, and mental health are not represented, even though they play critical roles in cardiovascular outcomes. The dataset is also region-specific (collected in Eastern Europe), which means its findings may not apply globally. And because the data is a snapshot in time, we cannot know how changes in lifestyle over years might alter risk. In the end, the impact of this project lies in its balance: it provides insights into the role of lifestyle in cardiovascular disease, but it also highlights the importance of being critical about data quality, representation, and limitations. With careful framing, this type of analysis can help raise awareness and encourage healthier choices, while also reminding us that no single dataset tells the whole story.

# 1) Convert age from days to years
df_proc["age_years"] = (df_proc["age"] / 365.25).round(1)

# 2) Derived features
df_proc["bmi"] = df_proc["weight"] / (df_proc["height"]/100)**2
df_proc["pulse_pressure"] = df_proc["ap_hi"] - df_proc["ap_lo"]

import numpy as np

def pair_count_heatmap(df, a, b):
    table = df.groupby([a, b]).size().unstack(fill_value=0).sort_index().reindex(index=[0,1], columns=[0,1], fill_value=0)
    data = table.values

    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(4,4))
    im = ax.imshow(data, aspect="equal")  # default colormap, no style set

    ax.set_xticks([0,1]); ax.set_yticks([0,1])
    ax.set_xticklabels([f"{b}=0", f"{b}=1"])
    ax.set_yticklabels([f"{a}=0", f"{a}=1"])
    plt.title(f"Sample sizes: {a} Ã— {b}")

    # annotations
    for i in range(data.shape[0]):
        for j in range(data.shape[1]):
            ax.text(j, i, f"n={data[i, j]}", ha="center", va="center", fontsize=10)

    plt.colorbar(im, fraction=0.046, pad=0.04)
    plt.show()
    print(table)  # also print the exact counts for reference
